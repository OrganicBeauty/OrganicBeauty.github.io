How Diet Affect Acne and Spots

The role of specific foods, such as dairy products, along with certain dietary patterns, may be a potential cause of acne and acne outbreaks.

When it comes to diet and acne, foods that quickly raise your blood sugar (also known as high glycaemic foods), as well as dairy, may play a role in the development of acne.

Consuming foods that are high in sugar, simple carbohydrates like starch, and dairy products can potentially elevate the levels of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) in your body. An excess of this hormone in your bloodstream can trigger an overproduction of sebum by your oil glands, consequently contributing to the occurrence of acne and inflammation.

Some researchers suggest that IGF-1 may also contribute to the increased production of dihydrotestosterone (DHT) and testosterone. These hormones bind to receptors in the skin, prompting the expansion of sebaceous glands and an increased production of oil, both of which play a role in the development of acne.

Foods that lead to rapid spikes in blood sugar levels include sugar, pasta, white bread, breakfast cereal, and white rice.

What foods to avoid with acne

While many of us grew up hearing how greasy junk foods could wreak havoc on our complexions (“oily food equals oily face”), the science shows it isn’t so simple. Several foods have the potential to trigger breakouts for a variety of reasons. If you suspect your diet may be contributing to your blemishes, consider taking a closer look at your consumption of these foods.

Milk and dairy products

Numerous studies conducted over the years have pointed to the adverse effects of dairy consumption on skin health. One prevailing theory suggests that proteins found in milk may activate hormones and growth factors within the body, thereby increasing the likelihood of acne development. Research consistently demonstrates that the consumption of any type of dairy product, whether it's full-fat dairy, whole milk, low-fat/skim milk, or yogurt, regardless of quantity or frequency, is linked to a higher probability of developing acne compared to individuals who abstain from dairy consumption. Nevertheless, it's important to note that most of this research establishes associations between dairy and acne rather than establishing a direct causal relationship. It remains unclear whether milk definitively triggers acne for the majority of people.

High-glycemic foods

The glycemic index (GI) is a metric that assesses the impact of carbohydrate-rich foods on blood sugar levels. Foods with a high GI are typically, though not exclusively, processed or refined carbohydrates such as white bread, white rice, sugary cereals, and snack items, among others. A diet that includes excessive sugar may contribute to the development of insulin resistance. These high-GI foods lead to a sharp increase in both your blood sugar and insulin levels (Holesh, 2021). Consequently, these spikes can activate various factors within your body, including insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), and pathways that heighten the likelihood of acne formation. Furthermore, the rapid elevation in blood sugar levels can also induce inflammation, exacerbating the presence of acne.

Chocolate

Nobody welcomes discussions that discourage the enjoyment of chocolate, even though it has long been suspected as a potential acne trigger. A number of studies have hinted at a possible connection between chocolate consumption and an increased likelihood of exacerbating acne. However, it remains uncertain whether this association is due to chocolate's high glycemic index, similar to other sugary foods, or if other factors come into play. Additionally, dark chocolate contains antioxidants that might offer some benefits for managing acne. In summary, there is currently no established cause-and-effect link between chocolate and acne, and further research is required to explore this topic in greater depth.

Fatty foods

It's hard not to acknowledge the fact that greasy and fatty foods are one of the main causes of acne. Studies indicate that the consumption of fried foods, oily dishes, fast food, and similar items may indeed worsen acne. Processed foods, in particular, can result in elevated blood glucose levels that are not easily processed by the body, potentially contributing to acne. One recent study revolves around the idea that saturated fats and trans fatty acids might prompt an increase in IGF-1 levels, which in turn could lead to the development of acne.

Healthy diet containing oats, whole wheat barley, etc can be a good alternative source to attain good nutrients and vitamins to avoid acne.
